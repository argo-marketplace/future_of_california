# Reform and Renewal

Why has California's reform movement failed to tackle the underlying structural challenges? And what would a genuinely transformative approach look like?

## The Copy-Paste Problem

Most reform efforts operate by copying what works elsewhere — taking something that succeeded in one context and transplanting it. This is going from 1 to N: replicating known solutions. It's valuable, but it's not enough for California.

California's challenges are not ordinary. A state constitution amended over 500 times. A budget process that lurches between feast and famine with a tax base dependent on capital gains. A governance structure built for 1879 running a $4 trillion economy in 2026. These problems don't have off-the-shelf solutions waiting to be imported from New Zealand or Estonia. They require pioneering the genuinely new — going from 0 to 1.

## Chesterton's Fence

Before tearing anything down, we should understand why it was built. G.K. Chesterton's principle: if you encounter a fence across a road and don't understand why it's there, don't remove it until you do.

California's governance structures — its overlapping jurisdictions, its initiative process, its two state water agencies, its baroque budget process — all exist for reasons. Some of those reasons are obsolete. Some are not. The reformer's first job is to understand which is which.

This is not an argument for inaction. It's an argument for informed action. The reform movement has too often swung between naïve enthusiasm ("just make government more like a startup!") and cynical despair ("nothing ever changes"). Both miss the mark.

## Metis: Practical Knowledge

The ancient Greeks distinguished between *techne* (technical knowledge, the kind you can write down) and *metis* (practical knowledge, the kind you can only learn by doing). California reform has been long on techne — policy papers, best practices, benchmarking studies — and short on metis.

The people who know how California government actually works — the career civil servants, the county administrators, the water district managers — possess metis that reformers ignore at their peril. The [[data-collaboratives|California Data Collaborative]] succeeded in part because it was built *with* water agency staff, not *for* them. The [[public-tech-principles|implementation strategies]] emphasize community involvement for the same reason.

## The Frontier Metaphor

California has always been a frontier. Not just geographically — the edge of the continent — but intellectually. The state's greatest achievements have come from embracing the frontier mentality: building things that had never existed before.

The Master Plan for Higher Education. The state water system. The environmental regulations that became the template for the nation. These were not copied from anywhere. They were invented here, by people who understood the specific conditions of this place.

The next generation of [[public-tech-principles|public technology]] requires the same frontier spirit. Not "what are other states doing?" but "what does California need that doesn't exist yet?"

## From Zero to One

The path forward requires both humility and ambition:

- **Humility** to learn why things are the way they are before proposing changes
- **Ambition** to build genuinely new institutions rather than settling for incremental improvements
- **Patience** to build durable capability rather than launching flashy initiatives
- **Courage** to consolidate obsolete structures when understanding confirms they no longer serve their purpose

California's reform movement needs fewer white papers and more working prototypes. Fewer conferences and more collaboratives. Less copying and more creating.
