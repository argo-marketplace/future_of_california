# Seeding the Second Foundation

An evolving series on improving government operations — building on activity in California, developments at the federal level, and experiments across our pale blue dot.

## The Story So Far

The California Adaptive Technology (CAT) initiative emerged as a response to the creation of the federal Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE). The framing was playful — CATs and DOGE — but the underlying argument was serious: California needs its own approach to government modernization, one grounded in building capability rather than wielding a wrecking ball.

That framing has since evolved. Both CATs and DOGE, it turns out, are working from the center of established institutions, albeit from different angles. The more interesting contrast is not between California and the federal government but between institutional reform from the inside and genuinely disruptive approaches from outside — what might be called the "Evil Morty Protocol."

## The Evil Morty Protocol

The reference is to *Rick and Morty*: Evil Morty is the character who doesn't try to fix the system or fight it. He leaves. He builds something new outside the existing structure entirely.

Applied to government operations, this raises an uncomfortable question: Can existing institutions be reformed from within, or do we need to build parallel structures that demonstrate what's possible and eventually replace what isn't working?

The answer is probably both. The [[public-tech-principles|Public Technology Principles]] represent the reform-from-within approach. The [[data-collaboratives|California Data Collaborative]] represents something closer to the parallel-structure approach — a new kind of institution (a data utility) that didn't exist before and couldn't have been created through traditional government channels.

## The Consultant Industrial Complex

One recurring theme in the [[california-counts/index|California Counts]] data analyses is the heavy reliance on external consulting. The GovOps Secretary's office spends nearly as much on consultants as on its own staff. This pattern — a thin layer of permanent government employees managing a rotating cast of contracted specialists — is not unique to California. It's the dominant model of modern government operations.

The "Second Foundation" metaphor points toward a different model: building durable institutional capability rather than renting it. This doesn't mean eliminating consulting. It means inverting the ratio — building enough internal capacity that consulting supplements rather than substitutes for government capability.
