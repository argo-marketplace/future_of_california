# California Counts: The Veto Count
## How many discrete approvals does it take to build a water recycling plant in California?

For this installment I stopped poking around Open FI$Cal and started counting something else: approvals. Specifically, the discrete set of permits, certifications, clearances, and authorizations that a representative California water recycling facility needs before a single shovel can turn. Not the dollar cost. Not the years. Just the count. Each approval is an independent yes, and therefore an independent potential no, which is what political scientists mean when they talk about veto points. By my count, a stylized 10-million-gallon-per-day advanced water purification plant with some federal funding touches at least 25 distinct approval processes administered by roughly 17 different governmental bodies before it is allowed to exist.

---

## The Topline: 25 Discrete Approvals

The reference project for this exercise is not a real one. It is a composite: a mid-sized advanced water purification facility, roughly 10 MGD of capacity, sited on an urban infill parcel near the coast, with partial federal funding through the EPA's Clean Water State Revolving Fund and some Bureau of Reclamation Title XVI money. It takes recycled water from an existing wastewater plant, treats it to drinking-water standards, and injects it into a groundwater basin for indirect potable reuse.

None of that is exotic. It is the workhorse configuration that every California water agency serious about drought resilience is trying to build right now.

Here is the permit stack.

Twenty-five approvals. Seventeen agencies, counting federal, state, regional, and local bodies as separate. If you add a DBE participation plan for the federal funding, a SWRCB intertie permit for the groundwater injection, a CPUC review because the project crosses into a franchise utility's territory, and a handful of municipal utility cross-connections, the count pushes past 30.

## What Counts as an Approval

Before the critical reader objects, a word on methodology. Not all 25 items are equal. A few of them are ministerial in principle. A building permit is supposed to be issued as of right once the drawings comply with the code. A Section 401 certification has a 60-day statutory shot clock, extendable up to a year. CEQA review is discretionary and can run four to seven years for a project of this size. The count flattens all of them to "one."

I'm flattening them on purpose. The point is not that every approval carries the same weight. The point is that each one is a procedurally independent yes administered by a body with its own staff, its own mandate, its own timing, and its own political accountability. Each is a place where the project can pause. Each is a place where a litigant can intervene. Each is a place where a new commissioner with new priorities can reopen settled questions.

That is the political science definition of a veto player, and it travels well from Tsebelis to the water district boardroom. Tsebelis's original finding was that as the number of independent veto players rises, the range of possible policy changes narrows. California's infrastructure permitting stack is the applied version of that theorem.

---

## The Comparison That Makes the Number Mean Something

Twenty-five approvals is a big number in isolation. It is an argument in comparison.

Take the same reference project to Texas. Texas has no state environmental review statute of its own; there is no CEQA analog. Recycled water is regulated by the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality under Chapter 210, which is a single permit that covers reuse, discharge, and water quality in one authorization. There is no state coastal commission with veto authority over the Gulf shoreline equivalent to what the California Coastal Commission holds over the Pacific. Labor compliance does not require a Skilled and Trained Workforce certification. Local zoning and building permits are handled by the municipality or, in unincorporated areas, by the county with limited review. Federal NEPA and Section 404 still apply if federal funding or wetlands are involved. The total approval count for an equivalent project falls to roughly 8 to 12, depending on exactly how the count is conducted.

Florida sits between the two. The Florida Department of Environmental Protection runs a consolidated permitting process that bundles water quality, wetlands, and stormwater into a single Environmental Resource Permit. Local Water Management Districts issue a separate consumptive use permit. Florida has a Growth Management Act and a coastal zone program, but both operate through the locally adopted comprehensive plan rather than through a standalone state commission with independent permit authority. Count: roughly 10 to 14.

Germany is the interesting European peer. Under the Bundes-Immissionsschutzgesetz, a project of this category goes through a single Genehmigungsverfahren, a plan approval procedure administered by the Länder environmental authority. The statutory design is explicitly one-stop. Water law, air emissions, nature protection, and construction authorization are all folded into a single decision subject to a single legal challenge. The German count is not really a count in the California sense. It is one decision with many inputs.

A cleaner way to state the comparison is in the table below.

| Jurisdiction | Approvals (stylized) | State environmental review analog | Coastal commission analog |
|---|---|---|---|
| California | ~25 | CEQA | Yes, with independent permit authority |
| Texas | ~10 | None | No |
| Florida | ~12 | Limited (ERP is consolidated) | Operates through local comp plans |
| Germany | 1 omnibus | Plan approval procedure | Rolled into plan approval |

The shape of the difference is not that California reviews things no one else reviews. Federal wetlands, endangered species, and historic resources get reviewed everywhere there is a federal nexus. The shape of the difference is that California reviews them through separate state-level bodies on top of the federal reviews, and then layers a state environmental review process (CEQA) on top of that, and then layers a coastal permit on top of that, and then requires the ratepayers to clear a Proposition 218 procedure before the money can be raised to pay for any of it.

The count is cumulative, not substitutive. Each layer was added for a reason. Each layer interacts with the others in ways that nobody planned.

## The Interpretive Tension

This is the place in a California Counts post where both readings get named honestly.

The critical read is straightforward: 25 independent veto points is not a regulatory environment, it is an obstacle course. Every approval has a cost. Every approval has a timeline. Every approval is a place where a project opponent can litigate, or delay, or run out the clock. The cumulative result is that California builds water reuse capacity at a pace that bears no relationship to the urgency of its drought exposure. Pure Water San Diego, a flagship project of this exact type, began its environmental review in 2011 and is scheduled to deliver first water in 2026, a 15-year span that includes multiple rounds of CEQA litigation. In Texas, the Big Spring direct potable reuse project was conceived in 2009 and delivering water by 2013. The comparison is not flattering.

The more generous read is that every one of those 25 approvals exists because a prior generation of Californians decided, for reasons that were often extremely good at the time, that the thing being approved mattered enough to require independent sign-off. CEQA exists because California was being paved over without environmental consideration. The Coastal Commission exists because the Pacific shoreline was being privatized into oblivion. The Division of Drinking Water exists because people used to get sick from their tap water. Proposition 218 exists because ratepayers were being charged without consent. Prevailing wage and the Skilled and Trained Workforce requirements exist because construction workers were being underpaid and undertrained. Each body in the stack has a constituency that can point to specific historical harms it was created to prevent. You cannot delete CEQA without losing what CEQA was built to provide.

Both readings are honest. They do not resolve each other. The count does not settle the argument. The count clarifies what the argument is actually about.

---

## What the Data Doesn't Show

The count is a stylized model, not a census. Real projects have different combinations of triggers. A project with no federal nexus drops seven or eight federal approvals from the stack. A project outside the coastal zone drops the CCC permit. A project in a brownfield adds DTSC oversight. My number is the base case for a federally co-funded, coastal, groundwater-injection water reuse project. It is plausible, not canonical.

The count also does not tell you about sequencing, duration, discretion, or compliance cost per approval. It is plausible that a regime with 25 fast parallel approvals would be less burdensome than a regime with 10 slow sequential ones. The count is a first-order measurement, and first-order measurements are where civic analysis of permitting should begin, not end.

And the count says nothing about what the approvals achieve. Some of them prevent genuine harms. Some of them collect fees and produce paperwork. The data cannot distinguish the two.

---

California did not set out to build a 25-gate permitting system for water reuse on purpose. It accreted, one reasonable approval at a time, over fifty years of legislative sessions and ballot propositions and federal consent decrees. What it becomes on paper is a list. What it becomes in practice is a political science result: more veto players, less building, longer horizons, fewer projects. The count is a way of naming the thing that was actually built. What to do about it is the harder question, and the one the numbers cannot answer on their own.

Comparative counts for Texas, Florida, and Germany are drawn from each jurisdiction's published permitting guidance and should be read as illustrative rather than definitive. A real project count in any of these jurisdictions will vary by site, funding source, and discretionary triggers. All underlying notes available on request.*
