# California Counts: The Emptying Hospital
## What two decades of utilization data reveal about where Californians actually get care

For this installment I left the budget spreadsheets behind and looked at hospitals. Specifically, at the utilization trend data published by the Department of Health Care Access and Information, the agency formerly known as OSHPD that licenses every hospital in California and collects the discharge records. The dataset covers inpatient discharges, emergency department visits, and ambulatory surgery volumes from 2005 through 2024. The headline finding is not the pandemic. The pandemic is the earthquake in the middle of the record. The headline finding is the structural shift that was underway before COVID arrived and accelerated after it left: California's hospitals are performing fewer inpatient surgeries, handling shorter stays, and watching a growing share of procedural volume migrate to outpatient settings. The hospital of 2024 is not the hospital of 2005. The data shows the transformation in progress.

---

## The Topline: A System in Transition

California's hospital system is large by any measure. In 2020, the state recorded 3.44 million total inpatient hospitalizations and 10.02 million emergency department visits across its licensed general acute care hospitals. Those are statewide figures reported by HCAI, and they represent the system at its pandemic nadir, not its peak.

The more revealing numbers are the trends on either side of 2020. Between 2015 and 2022, inpatient surgeries declined 15.9 percent statewide. Over the same period, outpatient surgeries increased 7.9 percent. The crossover is not subtle. Procedures that once required overnight admission, joint replacements, cardiac catheterizations, certain cancer resections, are migrating to ambulatory surgery centers and hospital outpatient departments where patients go home the same day. Medicare's removal of procedures from its "inpatient only" list, a policy change that accelerated in 2020 and 2021, formalized what clinical practice was already doing.

This is not a California-specific phenomenon. The shift from inpatient to outpatient is happening nationally. But California's version has a distinctive wrinkle: the state's emergency departments are absorbing a growing share of acute care that the inpatient side is shedding.

---

## The Emergency Department: California's Safety Net in Practice

The emergency department data tells a story that anyone who has waited six hours in an ED waiting room already suspects. California's EDs function as the de facto primary care system for millions of residents who lack access to timely outpatient care, lack insurance adequate for a specialist visit, or face a behavioral health crisis with nowhere else to go.

Los Angeles County illustrates the scale. In 2019, LA County hospitals recorded approximately 3.2 million ED visits. In 2020, that number fell to 2.4 million, a 25 percent drop driven by pandemic avoidance. Patients stayed home. Some of them died at home from strokes and heart attacks they would have survived had they come to the emergency department. By 2022, ED volumes had rebounded to near pre-pandemic levels, and by 2024 they were exceeding them in most regions.

The pediatric data is particularly stark. Children aged zero to 17 made 1.2 million fewer ED visits statewide in 2020 compared to 2019, a 43 percent decline. That number is not primarily a story about COVID in children, who were largely spared severe illness. It is a story about the visits that did not happen: the febrile infants not brought in, the asthma exacerbations managed at home, the fractures splinted by parents who were afraid of the hospital. Some of that reduction likely represented unnecessary visits that families had been making out of convenience or anxiety. Some of it represented genuine care foregone.

---

## The Inpatient Decline: Structural, Not Cyclical

Through 2022, total inpatient admissions remained approximately 4 percent below pre-pandemic levels even as the population continued to grow. Adjusted patient days, a metric that accounts for outpatient activity by weighting inpatient days against total revenue, exceeded pre-pandemic levels by 7 percent as of the fourth quarter of 2022. The gap between those two numbers is the outpatient shift in a single statistic: fewer people sleeping in hospital beds, but more total clinical activity happening under hospital roofs.

The inpatient decline predates COVID by at least a decade. Average length of stay has been falling since the 1990s, driven by clinical evidence, payer pressure, and the development of minimally invasive surgical techniques that turned what were once five-day recoveries into same-day discharges. California's average length of stay for a general acute care admission was 4.6 days in 2020, down from over 6 days in the early 2000s.

The practical consequence is that California has more licensed hospital beds than it needs for inpatient care and fewer than it needs for other things. The state licensed approximately 78,000 general acute care beds as of 2023. Occupancy rates for many community hospitals outside the major academic medical centers run below 60 percent. At the same time, psychiatric beds are in severe shortage, skilled nursing capacity is constrained, and post-acute rehabilitation slots are scarce enough that patients routinely board in acute care beds waiting for discharge placement.

The hospital is being asked to do less of what it was built for and more of what it was never designed to do.

---

## Rural Closure and the Geography of Access

The structural shift hits rural California hardest. Between 2010 and 2024, California lost more than a dozen rural and community hospitals to closure or conversion, joining a national pattern that has seen over 150 rural hospital closures since 2005. The economics are straightforward: a 25-bed critical access hospital with 40 percent occupancy cannot sustain the fixed costs of 24/7 emergency department staffing, laboratory services, and pharmacy on a payer mix dominated by Medi-Cal and Medicare.

HCAI's utilization data captures this indirectly. When a rural hospital closes, its emergency department visits and inpatient admissions do not disappear. They redistribute to the next nearest facility, increasing drive times for patients, increasing volumes at receiving hospitals, and increasing the number of patients who simply do not seek care because the barrier to access has become too high. The data shows the volume transfer. It does not show the patient who had a chest pain at home in Tehama County and decided it was probably nothing.

The state has responded with a series of programs: the Distressed Hospital Loan Program, which provided $150 million in emergency loans to hospitals at financial risk of closure; the Health Care Workforce Initiative; and ongoing Medi-Cal rate adjustments intended to narrow the gap between what the state pays and what care costs. Whether these interventions are sufficient to stabilize rural access is an open question that the utilization data frames but cannot answer.

---

## COVID as Accelerant

The pandemic did not create the trends visible in the utilization data. It accelerated them. The shift from inpatient to outpatient was underway. COVID compressed years of gradual migration into months of forced adaptation. Hospitals that had been slowly developing telehealth programs launched them overnight. Surgeons who had been gradually moving procedures to ambulatory settings were suddenly barred from performing elective inpatient surgery entirely during the spring 2020 surge restrictions.

When restrictions lifted, the old patterns did not fully return. Patients who had experienced a telehealth visit for a follow-up appointment did not all return to in-person visits. Surgeons who had demonstrated that a procedure could be performed safely in an outpatient setting did not move it back to the inpatient operating room. Hospitals that had learned to manage lower census did not rehire to their pre-pandemic staffing levels, a dynamic that connects directly to the workforce vacancy data examined in the previous California Counts installment.

The 2020 utilization numbers are therefore simultaneously the least representative year in the dataset and the most important one. They mark the inflection point after which the hospital system reorganized itself around a new set of assumptions about where care happens.

---

## The Interpretive Tension

The critical read is that California's hospital utilization data reveals a system optimized for a model of care that is disappearing. The state licenses and regulates hospitals as though inpatient care is the core product. Seismic retrofit mandates under SB 1953, which required all hospital buildings to meet stringent earthquake safety standards by 2030, have driven billions in capital investment into physical plants whose inpatient wings are running at 60 percent occupancy. Certificate of need requirements, Medi-Cal reimbursement formulas, and graduate medical education funding all assume a hospital-centric delivery model. Meanwhile, the actual trajectory of clinical care is toward outpatient, ambulatory, and home-based settings that the regulatory and financing framework was not built for. The utilization data shows a system whose infrastructure is pointed in one direction while its clinical activity moves in another.

The generous read is that the decline in inpatient utilization is, in significant part, good news. Shorter stays mean less hospital-acquired infection. Outpatient surgery means faster recovery. The migration of procedures out of inpatient settings reflects genuine clinical progress, not system failure. California's hospital system is not emptying because it is broken. It is emptying because medicine has gotten better at keeping people out of hospital beds, which is what medicine is supposed to do. The challenge is not the transition itself but the lag between clinical reality and regulatory structure, a lag that every healthcare system in the developed world is navigating.

Both readings coexist in the same dataset. The utilization trend is simultaneously a story of clinical progress and regulatory mismatch, and the data does not tell you which interpretation deserves more weight.

---

## What the Data Doesn't Show

The HCAI utilization dataset records volumes: how many admissions, how many ED visits, how many surgeries. It does not record outcomes. A hospital that discharges patients sicker and quicker may show declining length of stay as a sign of efficiency or as a sign of premature discharge. The data cannot distinguish the two.

The data also does not capture the care that happens outside hospitals. As inpatient volumes decline, ambulatory surgery centers, urgent care clinics, federally qualified health centers, and telehealth platforms absorb the displaced volume. HCAI collects data from licensed hospitals and ambulatory surgery centers, but the full picture of where Californians receive care requires integrating data from Medi-Cal claims, commercial insurance, and the Department of Managed Health Care, datasets that do not talk to each other easily.

And the data says nothing about quality. Two hospitals with identical utilization profiles may have vastly different outcomes. A decline in ED visits may reflect better primary care access or may reflect patients who have given up trying to get care. The numbers measure throughput, not health.

---

California built its hospital system for a world in which being sick meant being in a hospital. The utilization data shows that world receding. Inpatient volumes are down. Outpatient volumes are up. Emergency departments are absorbing functions they were never designed for. Rural hospitals are closing. The pandemic accelerated all of it. What remains is a regulatory and financial infrastructure calibrated to the old model, governing a clinical reality that has already moved on. The gap between the two is where the interesting policy questions live, and it is a gap the utilization data can measure but not close.
