# California Counts: The Homekey Math
## What $3.78 billion in housing grants buys, and what it doesn't

California's Department of Housing and Community Development awarded nearly $2.8 billion in housing and homelessness grants in calendar year 2025. That is a large number. It is also a number that sits in tension with a larger one: the state's estimated housing shortfall of 2.5 million units. The grant data from the California Grants Portal tells a specific and uncomfortable story about the ratio between money spent and homes produced. It is a story about what grants can do, what they cannot do, and what happens when a state tries to buy its way out of a structural crisis one competitive application at a time.

---

## The Topline: Homekey's 15,962 Homes

Homekey is the most visible housing grant program in California's recent history. Launched in 2020 as an emergency response to the pandemic, it used federal CARES Act and ARPA money, later supplemented by state funds, to acquire and convert hotels, motels, and other existing buildings into permanent supportive housing. The results across three funding rounds are specific: $3.78 billion awarded to 261 projects, creating 15,962 homes serving an estimated 175,000 Californians over the projects' lifetimes.

Those numbers are real accomplishments. Homekey moved faster than almost any state housing program in memory. The original round converted hotels to housing in months rather than the years that conventional affordable housing development typically requires. The program demonstrated that California could, when it chose to, compress timelines dramatically.

But the scale tells a different story. The 15,962 homes produced by $3.78 billion represent an average cost of roughly $237,000 per unit. That is less than half the cost of a typical new-construction affordable housing unit in California, which runs $600,000 to $700,000 in the Bay Area. Homekey achieved that cost advantage through acquisition and conversion rather than new construction. The program was fast because it skipped building. It bought buildings that already existed.

The question is what happens when you run out of motels to buy.

---

## Homekey+: The Next Phase

Proposition 1, passed by California voters in March 2024 by the narrowest of margins (50.2% to 49.8%), authorized $6.38 billion in bonds for behavioral health and housing. Of that, $2.145 billion was earmarked for Homekey+, the program's next iteration.

The early results are modest. As of September 2025, Homekey+ had awarded $284.6 million to 16 projects creating 825 homes. The implied cost per unit, roughly $345,000, is higher than the original Homekey average. This is consistent with the program shifting from hotel acquisition toward more renovation-intensive conversions and, in some cases, modular new construction. The pool of cheap-to-convert motels has thinned.

At the current pace of $284.6 million producing 825 homes, the full $2.145 billion would produce roughly 6,200 units. That is a meaningful number in the context of homelessness (the January 2026 point-in-time count found a 9% drop in unsheltered homelessness statewide). It is a rounding error in the context of 2.5 million missing housing units.

---

## The HHAP Pipeline

The Homeless Housing, Assistance, and Prevention Program (HHAP) operates on a different model. Rather than funding specific projects, HHAP distributes block grants to continuums of care, counties, and large cities, which then decide locally how to spend the money on shelters, services, outreach, and housing. Nearly $5 billion has been appropriated across all HHAP rounds. Round 6 allocated $760 million. Round 7 is planned at $500 million. A Tribal HHAP allocation of $28.5 million and a Homekey supplemental of $161.5 million round out the recent commitments.

HHAP is not, strictly speaking, a housing production program. It is a homelessness response program that includes housing as one of many eligible uses. Its grant structure reflects this: local jurisdictions have wide latitude in how they spend the money, and the outcomes are measured in people served rather than units built. The tension between HHAP's flexibility and its accountability has been a recurring theme in Legislative Analyst's Office reviews.

## The Rest of the Portfolio

Beyond Homekey and HHAP, HCD administers a constellation of smaller grant programs that collectively represent the state's conventional approach to affordable housing. CalHome released a $176.8 million Homeownership Super NOFA in 2024, plus an $18 million Tribal CalHome allocation. The federal Community Development Block Grant program distributed $34 million in 2024 (38 projects in 20 counties, serving 160,000 residents) and approximately $27 million in 2025. The Infill Infrastructure Grant program, designed to subsidize the unglamorous but essential infrastructure, roads, sewers, utilities, that makes housing sites buildable, was among the programs cut in the 2024-25 budget tightening.

That budget context matters. The 2024-25 spending plan authorized $1.8 billion in new one-time housing and homelessness funding. But it simultaneously cut $1.5 billion from previously authorized programs, including the Infill Infrastructure Grant, Veterans Housing, CalHome, and Foreclosure Intervention. The net new money was roughly $300 million. In a state where a single affordable housing project can cost $100 million, $300 million net buys very few buildings.

A partial accounting of the major programs looks like this.

| Program | Funding (cumulative or annual) | Homes produced or planned |
|---|---|---|
| Homekey Rounds 1-3 | $3.78 billion | 15,962 |
| Homekey+ (Prop 1) | $2.145 billion authorized; $284.6 million awarded | 825 to date |
| HHAP (all rounds) | ~$5 billion | Not primarily a production program |
| CalHome Super NOFA | $176.8 million | Homeownership assistance |
| CDBG (FY24) | $34 million | 38 infrastructure projects |
| Infill Infrastructure Grant | Cut in FY24-25 budget | Reduced pipeline |

## The Interpretive Tension

The critical read is arithmetic. California has spent or committed roughly $12 billion on housing and homelessness grants since 2020 through Homekey, HHAP, Prop 1 bonds, CalHome, and the various smaller programs. That money has produced approximately 16,000 Homekey homes, funded shelters and services for tens of thousands more, and subsidized an uncertain number of conventional affordable units through tax credits, infrastructure grants, and local match programs. Against a shortfall of 2.5 million units, the production numbers are structurally inadequate. The state would need to sustain Homekey-level production for over 150 years to close the gap through grant-funded programs alone. Grants cannot solve the housing crisis. They can only manage its symptoms.

The generous read is that the housing crisis was never going to be solved by grants, and nobody serious claims otherwise. The shortfall is primarily a market-rate production problem driven by zoning restrictions, CEQA litigation, construction costs, and local resistance to density. Grants address the tail of the distribution: the people who cannot participate in the market at any price. Homekey housed 175,000 Californians who would otherwise have been on the street. HHAP funded the outreach workers, the shelter beds, and the case managers who keep the crisis from becoming a catastrophe. The 9% drop in unsheltered homelessness reported in January 2026 is evidence that the money is doing something real, even if what it is doing is not building 2.5 million apartments.

The structural problem is that California uses the same tool, competitive grants, for both crisis response and system building. Homekey worked because it was designed as an emergency program with emergency speed. The conventional affordable housing pipeline, which requires applications to multiple funding sources, CEQA compliance, local land use approvals, prevailing wage certification, and design review, takes five to seven years from conception to occupancy. Grant programs that fund that pipeline inherit its timeline. No amount of additional appropriation changes the fundamental speed of the machine.

## What the Data Doesn't Show

The grants portal data records awards, not occupancy. A Homekey grant that was awarded in 2023 may represent a building that is fully occupied, a building still under renovation, or a project that encountered cost overruns and is seeking additional funding. The 15,962 homes figure counts units in the pipeline, not keys in the hands of formerly homeless Californians.

The data also does not capture the total public cost of producing an affordable unit in California. A single project may combine Homekey funds, Low-Income Housing Tax Credits (which are not grants and do not appear on the portal), local inclusionary fees, Community Development Financial Institution loans, and philanthropic contributions. The per-unit cost visible on the grants portal is a fraction of the per-unit cost visible on the balance sheet.

And the data says nothing about the places where housing grants were not awarded because no viable project was proposed, because the local jurisdiction did not apply, or because the zoning did not allow the use. The geography of grant awards is itself a map of local political will. That map does not appear in the dataset.

---

Homekey was, by the standards of California government, a miracle of speed. It converted hotels to housing in months. It proved that the state could move fast when it decided to. The program's own success raises the harder question: if California can build housing this fast under emergency authority, why does everything else take seven years? The grant data cannot answer that question. But it frames it precisely. $3.78 billion produced 15,962 homes through acquisition and conversion. The conventional pipeline, using the same dollars per unit, would have produced fewer homes over a longer timeline at higher cost. The difference is not the money. The difference is the process. And the process is a choice.
