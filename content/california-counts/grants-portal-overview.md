# California Counts: What $253 Billion in Grants Tells Us
## Poking around the California Grants Portal so you don't have to

Most people think of California state spending as a single budget document, debated once a year in Sacramento and signed by the governor. But a growing share of the state's actual footprint travels through a different channel entirely: grants. Competitive awards, formula allocations, loans, and disbursements flowing from state agencies to cities, counties, nonprofits, tribal governments, businesses, and individuals. The California Grants Portal, the state's centralized clearinghouse for grant opportunities, now reports more than $253 billion allocated through over 1,600 grants from 70 state departments. That number is large enough to be its own economy. It is also, until recently, a number almost nobody could verify.

---

## The Topline: A Portal That Grew Up Fast

The California Grants Portal exists because of the Grant Information Act of 2018, which required the State Library to build a single website by July 1, 2020, where the public could find all competitive and first-come grant opportunities offered by state agencies. AB 132, passed in 2021, added a reporting mandate: grantmakers must submit post-award data for any grant closing on or after July 1, 2022. The result is a dataset that is only four years old but already covers a remarkable breadth of state activity.

In fiscal year 2024-25, 55 boards, commissions, conservancies, councils, agencies, departments, and offices published 394 funding opportunities on the portal. That is a 14% decrease from the prior year's 455 opportunities. The number of applications submitted, 9,527, represents a functioning marketplace where state agencies post money and the rest of California competes for it.

The year-over-year decrease in opportunities is worth noting. It coincides with a budget cycle in which Sacramento cut $1.5 billion in previously authorized housing and homelessness programs alone, and trimmed or deferred spending across multiple agencies. Fewer grants offered is what a tightening budget looks like from the grantee's side of the table.

Yet portal traffic moved in the opposite direction: an 11% increase in visitors. More people looking at fewer opportunities. The prior year had seen a 58% surge in visits, driven by new listings from the California Energy Commission, the Department of Health Care Access and Information, the Department of Fish and Wildlife, HCD, the Department of Food and Agriculture, and Cal OES. Demand for state money, it turns out, is not sensitive to supply.

---

## The Shape of the Money

The $253 billion cumulative figure is a headline, not an analysis. It spans all fiscal years since the portal's reporting requirements took effect, and it aggregates wildly different kinds of spending: a $42 million long-duration energy storage grant to Camp Pendleton sits in the same dataset as a $50,000 community garden grant to a rural nonprofit.

The interesting question is not the total. It is the composition.

The portal's grant categories span environment, housing, education, health, transportation, public safety, and agriculture, among others. Grants can carry multiple category tags, which means the percentages don't sum cleanly. But the weight of the money tells a story about what California has decided matters enough to fund through competitive processes rather than through direct appropriation or formula allocation.

Three domains dominate. Housing and homelessness programs, administered primarily through the Department of Housing and Community Development, awarded nearly $2.8 billion in calendar year 2025 alone, a figure that reflects the combined weight of Homekey, the Homeless Housing Assistance and Prevention Program (HHAP), CalHome, and the Infill Infrastructure Grant. Energy and climate programs, anchored by the California Energy Commission and the California Climate Investments portfolio, have channeled $32.9 billion in cumulative cap-and-trade appropriations through FY 2024-25, with $3.1 billion appropriated in the current fiscal year alone. And community development, including the federal Community Development Block Grant program that HCD administers for smaller jurisdictions, adds another layer.

The remaining departments cover everything from fish hatchery restoration to arts council fellowships, from drought relief for small water systems to disaster preparedness for tribal communities. The long tail of California's grant portfolio is genuinely diverse. It is also genuinely fragmented.

---

## Seventy Departments, One Portal

The portal lists 70 departments as grantmakers. That number itself is an argument.

A state resident looking for money to weatherize a low-income apartment building could, depending on the funding source, apply through the California Energy Commission, the Department of Community Services and Development, the California Public Utilities Commission's Energy Savings Assistance Program, or a regional energy network funded by ratepayer charges that technically aren't grants at all. Each program has its own application, its own timeline, its own eligibility criteria, and its own reporting requirements.

The portal was designed to solve the discovery problem: put all the opportunities in one place so applicants can find them. It was not designed to solve the coordination problem: why do 70 different departments run separate grant programs that often target overlapping populations for overlapping purposes?

The 2023-24 annual report highlights the top agencies driving portal activity: CEC, the Department of Health Care Access and Information, Fish and Wildlife, HCD, Food and Agriculture, and Cal OES. Six agencies across six different cabinet-level structures, each with its own appropriation, its own grantmaking rules, and its own definition of what counts as a successful outcome.

## The Interpretive Tension

The grants portal is simultaneously one of California's genuine civic transparency successes and a mirror reflecting one of its chronic governance problems.

The generous read is real. Before 2020, there was no centralized place to find California state grants. Applicants relied on word of mouth, consultant networks, and direct relationships with agency staff. Rural jurisdictions and small nonprofits were systematically disadvantaged. The portal democratized access. The post-award data mandate under AB 132 added accountability. The daily-updated Statistics Dashboard and the bulk CSV downloads on data.ca.gov represent exactly the kind of open data infrastructure that makes public oversight possible. California built a thing that works.

The critical read is also real. The portal reveals a grantmaking apparatus of staggering complexity. Seventy departments. Hundreds of active programs. Application requirements that vary by agency, by program, and sometimes by funding round within the same program. The transaction costs of applying for state grants remain high enough that many small jurisdictions hire consultants whose entire business model is navigating the portal on behalf of cities that cannot afford a full-time grants office. The portal made the maze visible. It did not simplify the maze.

There is a deeper structural question. Grants are, by design, discretionary. They require applications, competitions, scoring rubrics, and selection committees. That is appropriate when the state is trying to identify the best projects among many candidates. It is less obviously appropriate when the state is trying to get money out the door fast, as it was during the pandemic, during wildfire recovery, and during the homelessness emergency. The tension between "competitive merit" and "speed of deployment" runs through the entire portal. It is not a tension the data resolves.

---

## What the Data Doesn't Show

The portal data tells you which grants were offered and, for recent fiscal years, who received them and for how much. It does not tell you what happened next. Whether the money was spent on time. Whether the project was completed. Whether it achieved its intended outcome. Post-award data is a financial record, not a performance record.

The $253 billion cumulative figure includes multi-year appropriations and may double-count funds that were authorized in one year, re-appropriated in another, and finally spent in a third. It is a measure of legislative intent, not of money actually hitting the ground.

And the portal captures only competitive and first-come grants. Formula allocations, entitlements, and direct appropriations to state agencies do not appear. The portal is a window, not a census.

---

The Grant Information Act was a small bill. It required one website and one database. What it produced, four years later, is something closer to an X-ray of how California actually distributes public money beyond the budget document: through hundreds of programs, dozens of agencies, thousands of individual awards, each with its own application and its own logic. The portal didn't create that complexity. It made it legible. Whether California does anything with that legibility is a different question, and one the data cannot answer alone.
