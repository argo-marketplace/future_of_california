# California Counts: One in Five Chairs Empty
## What a 21% vacancy rate tells us about the state's capacity to govern itself

For this installment I set aside the budget data and looked at the people. Specifically, at the gap between the people California says it needs and the people it actually has. The Legislative Analyst's Office reports that as of December 2023, the vacancy rate across California state government was 21 percent. One in five authorized civil service positions sat empty. That number has been climbing steadily: the vacancy rate in December 2015 was 13 percent. Over those eight years, the number of established positions grew 11.5 percent while the number of vacant positions grew 52 percent. The state has been authorizing new positions faster than it can fill them, and it has been losing existing staff faster than it can replace them.

---

## The Shape of the Gap

CalHR's 2023 Annual Census counted 224,613 civil service employees as of December 31, 2023, roughly 1.2 percent of California's employed workforce. In that same year, 17,310 permanent separations occurred, a turnover rate of 7.7 percent. Of those separations, 7,642 were retirements, representing 44.1 percent of all permanent departures. Nearly half the people leaving California state service were retiring, not quitting for better jobs. This is the demographic time bomb that has been visible for a decade: a workforce aging out, with no comparable pipeline of new hires replacing it.

The vacancy rate is not distributed evenly. The positions hardest to fill are the ones the state most urgently needs. Nursing, mental health, information technology, and engineering classifications have vacancy rates that dwarf the statewide average. A December 2024 State Auditor's report on healthcare staffing at state facilities found that the vacancy rate at Atascadero State Hospital had reached just over 30 percent, at Porterville Developmental Center it was 36 percent, and at Salinas Valley State Prison it exceeded 50 percent. Most of these vacancies were in nursing and mental health classifications.

At the Porterville Developmental Center, 87 percent of the hours worked by nurses between 2019 and 2025 were filled by contracted registry staff. Not 87 percent of vacancies. Eighty-seven percent of all nursing hours. The state facility is, in practical terms, a contractor-operated nursing operation with a government logo.

---

## The Price of Empty Chairs

Vacancies generate savings on paper. Each unfilled position means the state does not pay the salary, benefits, pension contribution, and overhead associated with that role. The State Auditor quantified this for the three healthcare facilities it reviewed: approximately $247 million in vacancy savings at Atascadero, $188 million at Salinas Valley, and $157 million at Porterville, from fiscal year 2019-20 through 2024-25. That is $592 million in unspent salary budgets across just three facilities over six years.

Those savings are not free. Registry contract staff cost the state more per hour than permanent employees, even after accounting for the state's benefit package. And in 2023, the state was ordered to pay monthly fines for noncompliance with court-mandated mental health staffing levels at its prisons. As of 2025, the California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation had accumulated more than $95 million in fines for failing to staff its mental health positions at levels the courts had required.

The arithmetic is striking. The state saves money by not filling positions. Then it spends more money hiring contract workers to do the same work at higher hourly rates. Then it pays fines for not having enough permanent staff. The net fiscal effect of the vacancy crisis is not savings. It is a more expensive version of the same workforce, delivered less reliably, with legal penalties on top.

---

## The Compensation Gap

The vacancy rate does not exist in a vacuum. CalHR's Total Compensation Survey compares state employee pay to the private sector and other government employers across 30 benchmark occupations. The 2024 survey covers 406 state classifications mapped to those benchmarks, representing roughly 79 percent of employees in the surveyed bargaining units.

The results vary by occupation. When accounting for the state's comprehensive benefits package, including pension and health coverage, the state's total compensation is at or above market for some positions. But when comparing wages alone, the state was at or above market for only 10 of the 30 occupations examined. For the remaining two-thirds, state wages lagged. A 2023 CalMatters analysis found that analyst positions, the workhorse classification of state government, paid 30 percent less in wages than equivalent private sector roles. Even with benefits included, the gap was 17 percent.

For IT professionals, the gap is wider. The private sector in California, which includes the most valuable technology companies on Earth, pays software engineers and systems administrators at levels that state salary bands cannot approach. The same dynamic applies to registered nurses, where private hospital systems in urban areas offer signing bonuses, flexible schedules, and per-diem rates that state facilities cannot match. The state's compensation structure was designed for an era when lifetime employment, pension security, and predictable advancement were competitive advantages. For a growing share of the workforce, particularly in the occupations where vacancies are highest, those advantages no longer clear the market.

---

## The Classification Maze

California's civil service system uses more than 4,500 distinct job classifications. That number is not a typo. It means the state maintains separate job descriptions, exam processes, pay scales, and eligibility lists for thousands of narrowly defined roles. The system was built over decades as each department, agency, and bargaining unit negotiated its own titles, its own minimum qualifications, and its own promotional pathways.

In November 2025, CalHR announced what it called the largest human resources streamlining effort in California in decades: a Generalist Class Consolidation that will collapse 67 classifications into eight, affecting more than 30,000 positions in Bargaining Unit 1 and related excluded roles. The goal is to replace department-specific job titles with broader classification families, making it easier for applicants to find and apply for positions, and easier for departments to hire and promote without navigating a thicket of classification rules.

The consolidation is scheduled to take effect January 1, 2026. It is a genuine reform. It also covers roughly 67 of 4,500 classifications. The remaining 4,433 are still out there, each with its own exam, its own eligibility list, and its own hiring timeline. A separate IT Class Consolidation is underway for technology positions, but the broader system remains largely intact.

The hiring process itself is part of the problem. Applying for a state job requires taking a classification-specific exam, achieving a score that places the applicant on an eligibility list, waiting for a department to pull from that list, interviewing, receiving a conditional offer contingent on background checks and reference verification, and then beginning a probationary period. The process from initial application to first day of work routinely takes months. For a registered nurse who can get hired at a private hospital in two weeks, or a software developer who can accept a private-sector offer in days, months is an eternity.

## The Interpretive Tension

The critical read is direct: a 21 percent vacancy rate is a staffing crisis that undermines the state's ability to do the things it promises to do. California authorizes positions for nurses, engineers, IT specialists, environmental scientists, and social workers, then fails to fill them. The classification system is so complex that CalHR itself is trying to dismantle parts of it. The hiring process is so slow that qualified candidates accept other offers before the state finishes processing their applications. Meanwhile, the state pays $95 million in court fines because it cannot staff its prison mental health units, and it spends more on contract nurses than it would on permanent ones. The vacancy rate is not a human resources problem. It is a governance capacity problem. Every empty chair represents a permit that takes longer to process, an inspection that doesn't happen, a patient who sees a contractor instead of a state employee invested in the institution.

The generous read is that the vacancy number overstates the crisis. Budget expansions routinely create authorized positions that take one to two years to recruit and fill, especially for specialized classifications. Some vacancy is structural: rural prisons and remote fire stations will always be harder to staff than Sacramento offices, and a certain baseline vacancy rate is normal in any large organization. The retirement wave is real but predictable, and CalHR's class consolidation and hiring reforms represent genuine institutional self-awareness. The state's benefits package, including pension, health coverage, and job security, remains genuinely competitive for many workers, particularly in non-IT, non-healthcare roles. The 21 percent figure is a measure of authorized positions, not of operational crisis. Some of those positions were authorized speculatively, funded in flush years, and never expected to be filled immediately.

Both readings have evidence. The truth is probably that the vacancy rate reflects both structural inflation of position authority and genuine difficulty filling critical roles, in proportions that vary by department and classification. The data does not distinguish the two.

---

## What the Data Doesn't Show

The CalHR Census reports the headcount. The LAO reports the vacancy rate. Neither tells you which specific vacancies matter most. A vacant administrative assistant position in a department with adequate clerical support is not the same as a vacant psychiatrist position in a prison under a federal court order. The count of vacancies is a blunt instrument that treats all empty chairs as equivalent.

The data also does not capture the quality of the people in the filled positions. A fully staffed department of underqualified employees is not better than a partially staffed department of experts. The vacancy rate measures quantity, not capability.

And the data says nothing about the shadow workforce. California state government relies extensively on contractors, consultants, and temporary staff who do not appear in the civil service headcount. Every vacancy that is "managed" through a contractor represents a position that is functionally filled but institutionally hollow. The person doing the work does not accumulate institutional knowledge, does not train the next generation, and does not stay when the contract ends.

---

The state of California employs 224,613 civil servants to govern 39 million people, regulate the fifth-largest economy in the world, manage 280,000 acres of state parks, operate 34 prisons, run the largest state university system in the country, and administer hundreds of billions in public spending. Whether that is enough people depends on what you think the state should do. What the vacancy data tells us is that the state itself thinks it needs roughly 284,000 civil servants, and it has 60,000 fewer than that. The gap is not a rounding error. It is the distance between the government California has authorized and the government California has actually built.
